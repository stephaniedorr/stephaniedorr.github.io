THE CHALLENGE

Busy lives make staying organized a challenge.

Much care, love, and attention goes into our fury friends. Grooming, boarding, and routine doctor’s visits ensure that our furry friends stay healthy. Sometimes things go wrong and we find ourselves looking into the eyes of an injured or ailing buddy. It’s important to be prepared for times of emergencies

THE SOLUTION

Streamline the organizational process.

Pet Port streamlines the search process, organizes medical records, and allows you to set vaccination and appointment reminders.

Research

Get to know our users.

In order to understand how pet owners currently handled their pet’s medical records, booked vet and grooming appointments, and managed the care and health of their pet I developed and distributed a survey across social media channels. This survey aimed to specifically find out:

  • Would the pet owner utilize emergency facilities?
  • What about on the weekend or after hours?
  • Can pet owners locate their closest emergency facility?
  • How many pet owners have their pet groomed regulary?
  • Can pet owners get to their pet's records quickly?
  • In 2016 Americans spent $66.75 billion on their pets.
  • In 2016 $22,25 billion went to veterinary services, supplies, & OTC meds.
  • 100% of respondents said they would utilize an emergency facility.
  • 39% stated they didn’t know where their nearest emergency was located.
  • 44% stated they could not put their hands on their pet’s medical records.
  • 15% of respondents said they regularly use a groomer.

The picture was clear, we spend money on our pets (sometimes big money) and we could use a little help connecting with quality veterinarian professionals as well as keeping our pet’s medical records handy and organized.

THE COMPETITION

Understanding the competition

Most of the users surveyed were depending on Google or Yelp to locate their nearest veterinarian facillity. While both these companies have a strong brand and following they fall short in delivering the customizations Pet Port would deliver (storing medical records, storing pet information, etc). The only competitor on the market that was targeting the same services as Pet Port, Dog Medical Agenda, was only servicing the dog-owning community. With the ability to store and share information to any pet owner, as well as search, bookmark, and review, Pet Port could easily mark it's own territory amongst the big dogs. BRAND PSYCHOLOGY

Selecting brand visuals

I applied color psychology to the desired product reaction words. Blue and green were, initially, the main design colors but, thru user preference testing, it became apparent the use of these two colors together distracted many users. Users clearly perferred the blue (professional, trustworthy, organized). Blue would be Pet Port’s primary color with green, yellow and red accent colors (the action red color is #ED1B2E - the same color as the American Red Cross logo.) Open Sans was selected for the typography for it’s clear lines and legibility.

THE LOGO

Creating the tag

The logo grew from a simple thought of “pet information.” Pet Port would be a place for owners to access their pet’s most vital information at a moment’s notice and what’s the most basic source of a pet’s information? Their tag! I reached down and looked at my own dog’s tag. It was shaped like a bone. After going thru several rounds of preference testing a final logo was selected.

USER TESTING

UX and UI testing

Using Balsamiq I created a series of low-fidelity wireframes that could easily be shared and tested. Thru the use of UsibilityHub’s preference and click testing, I pinpointed several problems. The most pressing issue was that users didn’t seem to know what Pet Port was. User education was going to be critical. The solution was a series of tutorials would later be styled with coach marks and a mascot.

In addition to need for education, the home screen was confusing and cumbersome to users. It was replaced with a navagation bar across the bottom of the screen.

Summary

Lessons Learned

Ultimately I ended up replacing a home screen with a consistent navigation bar, cutting product education screens from four to two, adding more detail to the search screens, modifying the filter options and changing the calendar. After a final round of navigation testing it was time to reflect on lessons learned. Pet Port was my first experience designing a location-type product. Lessons learned range from organization of materials to the usability of a map feature. Here are the lessons that stand out the most to me:

  • User-testing, user-testing, user-testing. When in doubt test.
  • Consistency in design is vital. Create a heiarchy of design.
  • Keep it simple. More is not always better.
  • Real estate is valuable. Every bit of the screen should have a purpose.
